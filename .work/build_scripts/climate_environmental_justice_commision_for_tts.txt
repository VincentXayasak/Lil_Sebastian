NARRATOR: Welcome back to The Local Minutes, where we do the homework so you don't have to. Today, the Climate and Environmental Justice Commission navigates a mountain of trash data, color-coded bins, and the existential dread of a compostable future.

BEN: Welcome everyone. Leslie, we have a doozy today. The Climate and Environmental Justice Commission is tackling the annual Solid Waste Report, and spoiler alert: our bins are being rebranded.

LESLIE: It is the great bin identity crisis, Ben. We’ve got new state laws, new colors, and the city’s favorite guest, Jennifer Gilbert, explaining why your trash might actually be a climate policy.

PATRICK: Wait, so the government is now in charge of the color of my garbage? I feel like I need a degree just to throw away a yogurt cup.

BEN: Stick around, Patrick. We’ve got the full breakdown on the solid waste report, the Environmental Recognition Awards, and the mystery of the missing May meeting.

SFX: gentle pen tap

NARRATOR: Act One: The state of the bin. Jennifer Gilbert, a twenty-year veteran of the city’s conservation efforts, took the dais to walk us through the 2026 Solid Waste Report.

LESLIE: Jennifer was very clear: this report is a data collage. She’s reporting on 2024 CalRecycle data, using 2025 hauling figures, all in the year 2026. It’s enough to make your head spin.

BEN: The big news is that Davis is killing it with organic waste diversion. They’ve been at it for ten years. But, there is a looming crisis: sewage sludge.

PATRICK: I’m sorry, did you say sewage sludge?

LESLIE: I did. Currently, the city uses dried biosolids from the wastewater plant to cover trash at the landfill. It’s technically recycling. But, new state definitions mean that starting with the 2025 data, that stuff counts as waste.

BEN: Which means our diversion rate is about to drop five to ten percent through no fault of our own. It’s just math, but it hurts the scorecard.

PATRICK: So we’re being punished for our own poop? That seems like a tough break for the environment department.

LESLIE: Jennifer’s other big headache is SB 1383, the Short-Lived Climate Pollutants Act. It mandates specific colors for bins. Recycling must be blue, organics must be green.

BEN: Jennifer admitted she was actually annoyed by this, because she had just bought a bunch of beautiful brown organics carts. Now they have to be green.

SFX: soft chair creak

PATRICK: I appreciate the frustration, but is it really that hard to tell the difference?

LESLIE: Well, Jennifer pointed out that sometimes the bins themselves end up inside the truck. She actually saw her own bin get eaten on her Ring camera.

BEN: It’s true. The contamination rate for organics in Davis is a staggering 1.3 percent. That is world-class clean.

PATRICK: I’m just impressed the city is watching the bins on Ring cameras. That’s some high-tech trash surveillance.

NARRATOR: Act Two: The awards, the awards, and the committee shuffle.

LESLIE: The commission moved to bring back the Environmental Recognition Awards for 2026, targeting a launch around Earth Day 2027.

BEN: But they had a spat over the screening committee. Staff wanted a heavy hand in the initial review, but Commissioner Jim pushed back.

LESLIE: Jim argued that the committee was too staff-heavy and might make the commission look like a rubber stamp. They eventually compromised to get more commissioners on the screening panel.

PATRICK: Smart move. If you’re giving out an award for saving the planet, you don't want it to look like a bureaucratic favor.

NARRATOR: Act Three: The numbers and the wrap-up.

BEN: Let's hit the key figures. The city is currently at 62 percent waste diversion. They are aiming for 75 percent, but the regulatory changes are going to drop that number down.

LESLIE: Batteries collected: 881 gallons. Contamination in organic loads: 1.3 percent. And we have 16,000 subscribers to the Greener Davis newsletter.

BEN: Plus, the commission officially canceled their May meeting because there’s nothing urgent on the docket. They’ll be back in June for the big Integrated Pest Management update.

PATRICK: I feel like I’ve learned more about trash today than in my entire adult life. My bin is blue, my compost is green, and I will never look at biosolids the same way again.

NARRATOR: Next time, keep your eyes on the Transportation Commission and the potential for a new liaison to bridge the gap between traffic and climate goals.

LESLIE: And remember: the trash is what you make of it. See you next time.
